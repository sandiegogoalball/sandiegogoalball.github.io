History of Goalball

From rehabilitation to the world stage.

The Origins (1946)

Goalball was invented in 1946 by Austrian Hanz Lorenzen and German Sepp Reindl. It was originally designed as a way to keep blind World War II veterans active and to assist in their physical and mental rehabilitation after returning from the front.

Unlike many other sports for the visually impaired, goalball was not adapted from an existing sport played by sighted individuals. It was created from the ground up specifically for athletes with visual impairments.

Global Recognition

The sport gained international attention throughout the 1950s and 60s. It was introduced as a demonstration sport at the 1972 Paralympic Games in Heidelberg, Germany. Due to its success and growing popularity, it became an official medal sport at the 1976 Paralympics in Toronto.

The women's event was added to the Paralympic program at the 1984 Games in New York. Today, goalball is played in over 100 countries and is governed by the International Blind Sports Federation (IBSA).

San Diego Goalball Roots

The San Diego County Goalball program was born out of a family's passion and necessity. Neal and Lori Meyers founded the organization over a decade ago when their son, Michael, was just 11 years old. Seeking a competitive outlet for Michael, who is visually impaired, they realized that if a local program didn't exist, they would have to build it themselves.

What started as a small family initiative has grown into a thriving community staple. Under the leadership of the Meyers family and dedicated coaches like Matt Boyle and Joseph Hamilton, San Diego Goalball has become a hub for athletes across the county. We have established permanent homes for the sport at the Stagecoach Community Center in Carlsbad and the Mission Valley YMCA in San Diego.

Over the years, our program has not only produced national-level talent but has also served as a vital social and athletic anchor for the visually impaired community in Southern California, proving that blindness is no barrier to high-intensity competitive sport.
