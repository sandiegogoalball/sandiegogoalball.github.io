About Goalball

The premier team sport designed specifically for the blind and visually impaired.

What is Goalball?

Goalball is a Paralympic sport played by two teams of three players each. The objective is to throw a ball that has bells inside of it into the opponent's goal.

Players use their bodies to block the incoming ball, relying entirely on their sense of hearing and tactile cues from the court lines. Because all players must wear "blackout" eyeshades, the sport is perfectly inclusive for athletes with varying degrees of visual impairment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about the game.

Players don't see the ball; they hear it. The ball contains bells that rattle when it moves. The game is played in total silence so athletes can track the ball's position and speed by sound alone.
To ensure fair competition, all players wear "blackout" eyeshades that completely block out light. This levels the playing field between athletes who are totally blind and those who have some residual vision.
The court lines are tactile. String is taped underneath the floor markings, allowing players to feel the boundaries and their position using their hands or feet.
An official goalball weighs 1.25 kilograms (about 2.75 pounds). It is made of hard rubber and is roughly the size of a basketball, but much denser and with holes to let the sound of the bells out.
While there is no intentional contact between opposing players (as they remain on their respective halves of the court), it is a high-impact sport. Players frequently dive onto the hard floor to block balls traveling at high speeds.

Key Rules of the Game

01

The Quiet Rule

Spectators must remain completely silent during play so athletes can hear the ball. Cheering is only allowed after a goal or during official breaks.

02

10-Second Rule

Once a team gains control of the ball, they have only 10 seconds to throw it back at the opponent's goal.

03

Eyeshade Penalties

Touching your eyeshades without permission from the referee can result in a penalty, as it could be seen as an attempt to gain a visual advantage.
